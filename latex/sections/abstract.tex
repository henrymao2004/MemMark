\begin{abstract}
\todo{Field-stamping provenance (TierMem, MemOS, enterprise
tagging) assumes the writer is honest, but in the most
forensically valuable setting — a memory snapshot leaks, is
distilled, or migrates across systems — the holder of the snapshot
\emph{is} the writer. Token-level and action-layer watermarks
likewise fail because the original output text and action
trajectory are gone before the memory leaves the system. We
present \memmark{}, which embeds attribution into the memory
system's own latent evolve decisions: at each internal LLM call we
elicit $K$ equivalent candidates with self-reported weights, then
keyed-pick via the distribution-preserving \agentmark{} sampler.
Each selection is committed into a Merkle log whose signed anchor
and per-leaf inclusion proofs live inside the snapshot itself.
\textbf{Headline:} on \locomo{} across \amem{} and \graphiti{},
\memmark{} achieves R3 (snapshot-only) bit\_recovery $= 1.00$,
versus $0$ for a \texttt{signed-metadata-only} baseline and $0.15$
for the wrong-key control. The watermark stays robust under nine
memory-lifecycle attacks (RQ4) and embeds 40 bits/session (RQ2).
The cost is a controlled $\sim$7\% relative F1 trade-off (RQ1),
comparable to text-layer watermarks (KGW reports 5–15\%) and far
preferable to the alternative of trajectory-bound attribution that
collapses to $0$ in forensic settings.}
\end{abstract}
